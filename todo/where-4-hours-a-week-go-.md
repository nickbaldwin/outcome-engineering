## TL;DR

* Product leaders lose about half a day a week to product updates.

* Roughly a third of that time is spent translating work for executives.

* Agentic workflows, designed with clear stages and human review, can give hours back without losing trust.

I recently ran a survey with product leaders to put numbers behind what many of us feel intuitively. The results were remarkably consistent.

Nearly 70 percent of PMs spend half a day each week preparing product updates. About 33% of that time is spent translating work for executives. Another 30% percent goes to chasing inputs. That translation number is the one that stuck with me. Almost a third of the time spent on updates is about reframing the same information so it makes sense to someone with a different vantage point. This information system was never designed end-to-end to be ai-native.

### The 5 Core Steps to Automate Product Updates

Here‚Äôs the workflow we used to illustrate and teach.

Every week, the process starts with a trigger. A scheduled reminder goes out, so updates don‚Äôt rely on memory or heroics. Inputs are gathered from the systems where work already lives: Jira, Slack, Docs, Notion. This is the most time-consuming step and the easiest place to apply automation.

Next comes synthesis. This is where AI helps, too. It summarizes progress, surfaces risk, and flags gaps, leaving you to make sense of the insights. Then the update is tailored. The same facts are framed for different needs. One version is designed to create clarity across the company. Another is designed to support executive decisions. Finally, the update is distributed, and follow-ups are handled once, not five times.

Human review sits after synthesis and before distribution because judgment and accountability matter. If you are putting incomplete or garbage data in, the system will not work.

Under the hood, the system is less complex than it sounds and does not require you to be a coder or technical expert to implement. Signals from multiple sources flow into a single input pack. That pack is processed by an LLM project with fixed rules and structure. Two outputs are generated from the same source of truth: a company-wide update and an executive brief.

Same data. Different framing. No rework.
